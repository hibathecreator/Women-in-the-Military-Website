WASP- Women Airforce Service Pilots


In 1942, Army Air Force General Henry R. Arnold requested the formation of the Women Auxiliary Ferrying Squadron (WAFS) and the Women’s Flying Training Detachment (WFTD). These organizations would perform domestic flights and operations which would allow more male airforce employees to enter combat. Nancy Love led WAFS which consisted of 25 experienced women pilots that were responsible for delivering planes from factories to military bases. Jacqueline Cochran led WFTD which recruited 25 women pilots with 200 hours of flight time and a commercial license. These women ferried, tested, and delivered planes for repair. They also performed check flights, towed targets for anti-aircraft gunnery practice, instructed male pilot cadets, and flew on searchlight tracking missions.

On August 5, 1943, the two programs were merged to form Women Airforce Service Pilots(WASP) which had Cochran as the director. In order to become a WASP, the applicant needed a civilian’s pilot license and they needed to pass the Army Air Corps physical exam. Of the 25,000 women that applied, only 1,102 were accepted and they became the first American woman to fly military aircraft. Overall, WASP flew over 60 million miles while performing flight checks, towing cargo, transporting new planes to military bases, along with other jobs. By 1944, the WASP had flown every military aircraft, yet they were not given military status and the 38 that died on active duty were not even honored with military benefits or burials. General Arnold and Cochran went to Congress and proposed the bill that WASP be considered women’s service within the US Army Air Force but their bill was denied.

By the summer of 1944, the war was coming to an end and flight training programs were shutting down, which meant that more male civilian instructors were losing their jobs. In order to avoid being drafted to the ground army, they lobbied that they should be given the jobs done by the WASP. On December 20, 1944, the WASP was disbanded and the courageous women that had valiantly served their country were sent home without any recognition or praise. Reports about the WASP were sealed away like other military records which meant that they were made available to researchers until 30 years after. Unlike other stories from World War II, the sacrifices and accomplishments of the WASP were forgotten until the 1970s.

Then, in 1976, the Air Force began to admit female pilots into their program and they said it was the first time they were allowing women to fly their spacecraft. This statement infuriated many former WASP like Lillian Yonally who had served in the WASP base in California for more than a year. "It was impossible for anybody to say that. That wasn't true. We were the first ones," Yonally says. The fact that they were forgotten united former WASP and together, they lobbied to Congress so that they could be granted military status. In 1975, Bernice Haydu, the leader of the WASP movement, proposed a bill to the senate that WASP members should be given retroactive veteran status and after 2 years of lobbying, the bill was finally passed. Then, on March 10, 2010, the WASP received the Congressional Gold Medal, the highest civilian award, for their service and ‘revolutionary reform in the Armed Force’ during World War II. About 200 WASP, many in their eighties and nineties, arrived in the capital and were given the honor by President Barack Obama. The WASP were finally awarded for fighting anti-feminist attitudes and hateful stereotypes in order to serve their nation the best they could. In fact, they performed so well that in the graduation ceremony of the last WASP training class, General Arnold said that when the program first started, he wasn’t sure "whether a slip of a girl could fight the controls of a B-17 in heavy weather." But the WASP proved him wrong so he admitted that, "now in 1944, it is on the record that women can fly as well as men".
